# Example Posts — Cross-Archetype Voice Calibration

Six examples, each pinned to a specific archetype + tone combination. Read these before drafting when the user's persona matches one of these archetype combinations. They're voice-calibration anchors, not templates to copy.

---

## Example 1 — Sage / matter-of-fact, serious
*Archetype combo: Sage dominant, Creator secondary. Tone: 2-2-2-2.*
*Post type: Thought-leadership.*

> Most retention-curve analyses are disaggregated on the wrong axis.
>
> The default is to split by acquisition channel. Paid, organic, referral. It's the one everyone has dashboards for.
>
> The more useful split, in my experience, is by *intent at signup* — what the user said they came here to do. A user who came to try a feature looks completely different at day 30 than a user who came to solve a specific problem, even if they arrived through the same channel.
>
> The median product team doesn't capture intent at signup. So they can't see it.
>
> Two caveats: (1) intent is self-reported and therefore noisy; (2) for products where the job-to-be-done is narrow, the split collapses. For consumer products and horizontal SaaS, it often doesn't.
>
> What I'd want to see: more teams adding a single question at signup and cohorting their retention curves by the answer. My prediction is that the split reveals patterns that channel cohorting hides.
>
> Pushback welcome. Especially from folks who tried this and found it didn't help — those cases would update me.

*Why this works:*
- Opens with a specific claim, not a hook.
- Names the alternative and explains why it's better with a single mechanism.
- Concedes two limitations.
- Ends with an invitation to disconfirm.
- Zero humor, zero casual language, zero intensifiers. The voice *is* the argument.

## Example 2 — Hero / enthusiastic, direct
*Archetype combo: Hero dominant, Caregiver secondary. Tone: 3-2-3-3.*
*Post type: Work.*

> Four weeks ago, I told my team we'd ship the new onboarding flow by April 30.
>
> Two weeks ago, I was certain we wouldn't.
>
> The third week, something shifted.
>
> We'd been trying to ship a fully redesigned flow. Six screens, new data model, new analytics, new copy. The scope was real. We were about 60% done and I couldn't see the last 40% without a miracle.
>
> I went back to the team with a different framing. What's the smallest version of this that tests the hypothesis we actually care about? Forget the six screens. What's the experiment?
>
> Turns out it was two screens. Same hypothesis. Same analytics. Shippable in four days.
>
> We shipped yesterday. Two screens instead of six. The retention metric we're testing will read the same way either way.
>
> The lesson isn't about scope cutting. I've cut scope a hundred times. The lesson was that my team didn't need me to push harder — they needed me to change what we were pushing toward.
>
> Proud of them. On to the next one.

*Why this works:*
- The hook sets stakes without hype.
- The turn is specific — a *reframe*, not just hustle.
- Credits the team in a concrete way.
- Lands on a principle that's about the leader, not the team. (Hero: "I learned X.")

## Example 3 — Jester / casual, irreverent
*Archetype combo: Jester dominant, Outlaw secondary. Tone: 4-4-4-3.*
*Post type: Thought-leadership.*

> LinkedIn productivity advice is a loop.
>
> Someone posts "5 habits of high performers." It goes viral. 300 other people write versions of it. The habits are:
>
> 1. Wake up early
> 2. Read a book a week
> 3. Journal
> 4. Cold shower
> 5. One more thing, it doesn't matter, everyone forgets it
>
> You know who does those things? Nobody. No one I actually know who is high-performing does all five of those. Most of them do maybe one, because the thing they actually do for eight hours a day is the *work*, which is mostly not charming to post about.
>
> Here's what I'd pay to read instead: one concrete decision you made last week that you were unsure about, and what you'd do differently now. That's it. That's the post.
>
> If you want to follow me on LinkedIn and this is what you expected from me, I apologize. But also not really. The five-habits posts are why I stopped lifting cold showers.

*Why this works:*
- Punches ideas, not people.
- Joke has a claim underneath (most advice is performance, not substance).
- Self-deprecating close that doesn't undermine the claim.
- Notice the short staccato paragraphs — Jester-casual moves fast.

## Example 4 — Caregiver / formal, enthusiastic
*Archetype combo: Caregiver dominant, Sage secondary. Tone: 2-2-2-3.*
*Post type: Educational.*

> If you're a first-time engineering manager and someone on your team has just given notice, here is the week I wish I had known how to run.
>
> **Day 1 — Listen.** Your first conversation is not about retention, counter-offer, or what went wrong. It is about making sure the person feels heard. They have already made their decision. Trying to undo it in the first conversation damages trust.
>
> **Day 2 — Understand.** In a second, separate conversation, ask what they learned in this role and what they are looking forward to. This is not a retention play. It is a contribution to their next chapter, which also gives you information for your own growth.
>
> **Day 3 — Plan the transition.** Map what they own. Decide what gets handed off, what gets dropped, what gets documented. Communicate to the team transparently but respectfully.
>
> **Day 4 — Tell the team.** Do this with the departing person in the room if they are comfortable. Lead with gratitude and specifics. Avoid stock phrases.
>
> **Day 5 — Care for the rest of the team.** Someone on your team will be quietly wondering if they should also be looking. Check in individually, without making it about the departure.
>
> The instinct most new managers have is to fix. To retain. To explain the team's great future. That instinct is rarely what is needed in the first week. What is needed is presence, clarity, and respect for the person's decision.
>
> If you are in this situation this week, I am rooting for you. It gets easier with practice.

*Why this works:*
- Service-oriented. The post exists to help, not to demonstrate the author's competence.
- The structure is a gift — a reader can save and use it.
- Formal register (notice the absence of contractions) carries care rather than coldness.
- Close earns its warmth by not trying too hard.

## Example 5 — Everyman / casual, matter-of-fact
*Archetype combo: Everyman dominant, Explorer secondary. Tone: 4-2-3-2.*
*Post type: Personal.*

> Took my kid to the library last Saturday.
>
> He's four. He picked a book about dinosaurs that was, I think, objectively too long and boring for a four-year-old. I tried to steer him to a shorter one. He wasn't having it.
>
> We sat on the floor and I started reading it to him, skipping paragraphs, cutting sentences, trying to make it feel shorter.
>
> He stopped me. "Read the whole thing, Dad."
>
> So I did. All 34 pages. He sat through the whole thing. Asked questions about the long words I thought he wouldn't sit through.
>
> On the walk home I was turning this over. A lot of my job involves deciding what I think people can handle — customers, reports, stakeholders. I shorten things. I skip paragraphs. I try to make it feel easier.
>
> My kid reminded me that the assumption is often wrong. A lot of the time, people can handle more than I give them credit for.
>
> I'm going to try less skipping this week.

*Why this works:*
- A real, specific, low-stakes moment.
- The work connection isn't forced — it arrives naturally.
- No neat moral. The close is a concrete, small commitment.
- Matter-of-fact tone: no "it hit me like a lightning bolt" — just "I was turning this over."

## Example 6 — Creator / casual, enthusiastic
*Archetype combo: Creator dominant, Magician secondary. Tone: 3-3-3-4.*
*Post type: Work.*

> This is version 84 of a signup flow.
>
> Versions 1 through 18 were me trying to cram every feature onto the first screen. Classic first-time-founder move.
>
> Versions 19 through 40 were the "less is more" correction. I stripped the form down to email and password. Clean. Minimalist. Conversion dropped.
>
> Versions 41 through 60 were the research phase. I actually watched users use the flow. I read their Slack messages to our support team. I noticed that the minimalist version felt *untrustworthy* to some people — they wondered what they were signing up for.
>
> Versions 61 through 83 were putting the substance back, carefully. Not every feature. Just the ones that answer "what am I signing up for?" in the first three seconds.
>
> Version 84 shipped yesterday. It has a form with four fields, a one-sentence value prop, and a tiny block of text answering the three most common pre-signup questions.
>
> Conversion is up 34% over the minimalist version.
>
> The lesson I take from this — again, because I keep relearning it — is that clean and empty are different things. Simplicity is not the absence of content. It is the absence of unnecessary content. Those are not the same.
>
> I already have ideas for version 85.

*Why this works:*
- The version-number structure *is* the post. The Creator's obsession with iteration is the voice.
- Specific numbers.
- The arc is a journey — five phases, each with a learning.
- Close looks forward (Creator always looking to the next version).

## Cross-cutting patterns

Across all six examples, notice:
- **No clichéd openers.** No "Excited to share," no "I'm thrilled to announce."
- **Specifics carry the weight.** Numbers, scenes, phrases, decisions. Not abstractions.
- **Each voice is recognizable from one paragraph in.** You couldn't swap the Jester post's voice for the Caregiver post's voice.
- **Closes vary.** Some question, some reflect, some commit to an action, some just stop. The close is part of the voice, not a formula.
- **Hashtags are sparing.** Zero to three. No engagement bait.
- **Length varies by type.** Educational is longer. Personal is shorter. Neither is padded.

Match the archetype + tone first. Then write.
