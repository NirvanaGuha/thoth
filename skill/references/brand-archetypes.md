# Brand Archetypes — The 12 Jungian Types

Based on Carol S. Pearson and Margaret Mark's synthesis of Jung's archetypes for brand and personal voice work. These are not personality labels — they're **motivational patterns** that shape *why* someone speaks and *what they're reaching for* when they do.

During onboarding, Thoth pins a **dominant** archetype (drives ~70% of posts) and a **secondary** (shades the remaining ~30% — gives the voice dimension). Pinning both is important: a pure single archetype sounds flat.

---

## The four orientations

The 12 split into four groups of three, each oriented around a core human motivation:

| Orientation | Motivation | Archetypes |
|---|---|---|
| **Ego** — leave a mark | Provide structure | Hero, Outlaw, Magician |
| **Soul** — connect | Pursue spiritual fulfillment | Everyman, Lover, Jester |
| **Self** — find meaning | Pursue knowledge | Caregiver, Creator, Ruler |
| **Order** — provide structure | Leave a legacy | Innocent, Sage, Explorer |

*(Pearson's original grouping; the names of the orientations vary across editions — keep the archetypes, the grouping is a teaching tool.)*

## The 12, with LinkedIn-voice translations

### 1. The Hero
**Core drive:** Prove worth through courage and competence.
**LinkedIn signature:** Triumph over a specific, named challenge. Opens with stakes, closes with a lesson earned through struggle.
**Sounds like:** "The moment the demo broke, I realized the last six months were about to be decided in twenty seconds."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Arrogance, constant self-aggrandizement, fake underdog stories.
**Famous voices in this mode:** David Goggins, early Elon Musk, Kobe Bryant's "Mamba Mentality" essays.

### 2. The Outlaw (Rebel)
**Core drive:** Break what doesn't work. Disrupt.
**LinkedIn signature:** Contrarian takes on industry orthodoxy. Names the emperor's missing clothes. Low patience for buzzwords.
**Sounds like:** "Everyone's writing about AI agents. Almost no one is shipping them. Here's why."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Nihilism, hot takes for the sake of hot takes, dunking without building.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Paul Graham at his sharpest, David Heinemeier Hansson, Naval's more contrarian threads.

### 3. The Magician
**Core drive:** Make vision real. Transformation.
**LinkedIn signature:** Reveals how something works — the craft, the system, the invisible mechanism. "Here's the trick."
**Sounds like:** "People think copywriting is about words. It's actually about noticing what the reader is already telling themselves."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Mysticism, gatekeeping, "secret sauce" gimmicks.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Rory Sutherland, Derek Sivers, Jonah Berger on contagiousness.

### 4. The Everyman (Regular Guy)
**Core drive:** Belonging. Don't pretend.
**LinkedIn signature:** Honest about what's hard. Skips the polish. "Same as you."
**Sounds like:** "Shipped a feature last week. Two bugs in production within 48 hours. Here's what I learned about my QA process."
**Shadow side if overdone:** False humility, compulsive relatability, small thinking dressed as authenticity.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Seth Godin when he's being plainspoken, most great engineering managers.

### 5. The Lover
**Core drive:** Connection, beauty, passion.
**LinkedIn signature:** Writes about what they love — people, craft, aesthetics. Warm. Uses sensory language.
**Sounds like:** "The first time I saw a chart that made a six-month-old question make sense in three seconds, I remember thinking: oh, this is what good design is."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Saccharine, over-shares, performative warmth.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Paul Jarvis, Frank Chimero, Robin Sloan.

### 6. The Jester
**Core drive:** Lightness, play, truth through humor.
**LinkedIn signature:** Funny. Self-deprecating without being weak. Often makes a serious point by refusing to be serious.
**Sounds like:** "I spent three hours on a pitch deck. The slide that closed the deal was a screenshot of a spreadsheet I made in twelve minutes."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Never says anything real, hides behind jokes, the class clown who can't commit.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Nick Kolenda, Alex Lieberman (Morning Brew), Daniel Murray when he's riffing.

### 7. The Caregiver
**Core drive:** Protect and help others.
**LinkedIn signature:** Service-oriented. Writes to help. Spots what their audience is struggling with and offers real aid.
**Sounds like:** "If you're a first-time manager and someone on your team just quit, here's what I wish I'd known when it happened to me."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Martyrdom, saviorism, resentful helping.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Lara Hogan, Camille Fournier, Julie Zhuo.

### 8. The Creator
**Core drive:** Make something of lasting value.
**LinkedIn signature:** Obsessed with craft. Talks about process. Shows the work, not just the result.
**Sounds like:** "Version 47 of this landing page. Here's what changed between 1 and 47 and why most of it had nothing to do with the copy."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Perfectionism, navel-gazing about process, more talk than shipping.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Austin Kleon, Rick Rubin, Jessica Hische.

### 9. The Ruler
**Core drive:** Create order. Lead. Be responsible for outcomes.
**LinkedIn signature:** Strategic. Thinks in systems. Writes with the authority of someone who has been accountable for results.
**Sounds like:** "Three decisions we made in Q2 that we regretted by Q3, and the one process change that prevented them in Q4."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Authoritarianism, name-dropping, status-seeking.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Ben Horowitz, Cal Newport on work systems, Patrick Collison.

### 10. The Innocent
**Core drive:** Find simple happiness. See the good.
**LinkedIn signature:** Optimistic without being naive. Finds wonder in ordinary things. Clean, unguarded tone.
**Sounds like:** "Twelve years in and I still feel a small thrill when a deployment goes clean. I don't want to stop feeling that."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Toxic positivity, ignoring hard truths, cringey "gratitude posts."
**Famous voices in this mode:** Patagonia's writers, Robin Sloan on the Tinyletter era, many pediatricians' posts.

### 11. The Sage
**Core drive:** Understand the world through truth and knowledge.
**LinkedIn signature:** Analytical. Cites evidence. Corrects misconceptions. Slow before confident.
**Sounds like:** "Most of what you've heard about retention curves is wrong. Here's what the actual data shows when you disaggregate by cohort week."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Pedantry, "well actually," cold, superior.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Andrew Chen, Benedict Evans, Matt Levine.

### 12. The Explorer
**Core drive:** Discover. Freedom. Don't be fenced in.
**LinkedIn signature:** Writes from the road — literal or metaphorical. Curious, restless, keeps finding new terrain.
**Sounds like:** "Spent the last month talking to 40 customers on three continents. The pattern that surprised me wasn't the one I went looking for."
**Shadow side if overdone:** Restlessness, commitment-phobia, always-pivoting.
**Famous voices in this mode:** Tim Ferriss, Chris Guillebeau, Lenny Rachitsky.

## How to pin an archetype in the interview

The interview asks 6 questions. Read `onboarding-interview.md` for the exact script. During interview scoring:

- Count how many answers correlate with each archetype. The top is dominant; the next-highest distinct-orientation one is secondary.
- **Always pick from two different orientations** for dominant + secondary. A Hero + Magician blend (both Ego) tends to feel monomaniacal. A Hero + Caregiver (Ego + Self) blend is richer.
- If two archetypes tie for dominant, pick the one the user was **more energetic** about when discussing — archetypes are about drive, not just fit.
- Confirm with the user: *"Reading you as a dominant [X] with [Y] shading. That track?"* Let them adjust.

## How to sound like an archetype — practical mechanics

| Archetype | Opens posts with | Signature move | Close pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hero | A moment of stakes | Specific, scene-level struggle | Lesson earned the hard way |
| Outlaw | A named orthodoxy being challenged | "Here's what's actually true" | Invite dissent |
| Magician | An invisible mechanism | Reveal the trick | "Notice this and you'll see it everywhere" |
| Everyman | A small, specific admission | Plain fact, no flourish | Relatable close, no neat bow |
| Lover | Sensory detail, a moment of care | Name the beauty in the craft | Gratitude without schmaltz |
| Jester | A setup that feels serious | Punchline that reframes the serious point | Joke that's really a claim |
| Caregiver | The audience's pain, not theirs | Step-by-step help | Offer to go deeper |
| Creator | Version number, iteration, tool | Show the work | What they're making next |
| Ruler | A decision made under uncertainty | System-level reframe | Principle, not anecdote |
| Innocent | Ordinary moment, seen fresh | Gentle wonder | Small thanks, no BIG CAPS |
| Sage | A misconception or misreading | Cited or carefully-reasoned correction | "What I'm still uncertain about is..." |
| Explorer | A place, a crossing, a shift | Pattern spotted across the map | The next unknown |

## Anti-patterns by archetype

Each archetype has a default failure mode. Watch for it during voice checks:

- Hero → humble-brag
- Outlaw → empty contrarianism
- Magician → vague mysticism
- Everyman → manufactured authenticity
- Lover → saccharine
- Jester → jokes that say nothing
- Caregiver → martyr tone
- Creator → process porn
- Ruler → status signaling
- Innocent → toxic positivity
- Sage → pedantic "well actually"
- Explorer → restless namedropping of places/people

If the draft triggers the anti-pattern, rewrite.
